Mini Metal Foundry

A small foundry capable for melting down aluminum, copper, brass and similar metals.

Planning
One of my earliest projects was the idea of making something capable of melting down aluminum and various other metals. As I researched some of the best methods of doing this I stumbled across the designs and guides of a youtuber named The King of Random. After looking at his homemade foundry I decided that my design would be similar to his. The main differences would be that while he implored a charcoal method for heating the forge I would use a propane torch. With this in mind I decided to make the insulated walls out of a sand and plaster of paris mix all contained within a large steel trashcan. The lid would also be made using this mixture and will be cast in a donut shape to allow for air to flow out as well as metal to be inserted into the foundry. I also planned on using a clay-graphite crucible to actually hold the molten metal.

The King of Random's mini metal foundry



Prototyping
After successfully constructing an initial foundry out of a sand and plaster of paris mix I discovered several flaws with the design. The greatest weakness with the design was the durability of the lid and insulation. The foundry walls constantly cracked and the lid itself would constantly need to be recast due to it breaking from the heat. The foundry successfully melted the metal I desired but the durabilty of the foundry was less than desirable. Additionally the large opening allowed for the forge to have air to run but allowed for a lot of heat to escape. Overall it was a good prototype, but not something I would use in the long haul.

The main issue with the foundry was the durability caused by the plaster of paris and sand mixture. A new foundry would need to use a different form of insulation in order to maintain a desirable durability. With some research I decided that a new foundry would utilize kale wool as the primary insulation. The interior of the foundry would also have a line of firebricks to help heep the crucible stable and allow for spilled molten metal to not mix with the kale wool. The lid of the foundry would be the lid of a trashcan also lined with kale wool and two small holes for air to enter/exit the foundry.

The first attempt at a metal foundry



Finishing Touches
The new metal foundry turned out great and held up well with time. The new lid allowed for the foundry to heat up much quicker and has not needed to be remade at all unlike the previous iteration. Overall I am very happy with how the foundry turned out. Should this version ever need to be remade I would likely used satanite as an insulation material instead of kale wool. This material is far stronger and is rigid meaning that a new foundry wouldn't require fire bricks at all. This would allow for a highly durable and safer foundry than the one that I am currently using.
